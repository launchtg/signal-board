# EXAMPLES.md

This file shows what strong and weak Signal Board records look like.

## Example 1 — Strong Ideas Card

### Why it is strong
A strong Ideas card has:
- a clear buyer
- a recurring workflow problem
- visible market pain
- ugly manual workarounds
- obvious monetization
- a plausible MVP

### Example pattern
`RepAlert` is a good Ideas-stage example because:
- the buyer is obvious: local agencies / multi-location businesses
- the pain is recurring: review monitoring happens daily
- the workaround is ugly: manual checking across multiple platforms
- the cost of failure is real: bad reviews hurt revenue and reputation
- incumbent tools are expensive
- an MVP can be narrow and useful quickly

### What makes the record strong
- concise pain statement
- useful tags
- clear pricing hypothesis
- details include workflow, friction, evidence, APIs, why they'd pay, and bad alternatives

## Example 2 — Weak Ideas Card

### Why it is weak
A weak Ideas card often has one or more of these problems:
- buyer is vague
- pain is interesting but not painful
- no repeated evidence
- existing tools already solve it well
- monetization is weak
- MVP is too broad

### Example anti-pattern
A bad example would be something like:
- "AI assistant for general life optimization"

Why it is weak:
- no clear buyer
- too broad
- unclear urgency
- no obvious wedge
- hard to validate honestly
- crowded market with vague differentiation

Do not add cards like this just because they sound ambitious.

## Example 3 — Strong Design Card

### Why it is strong
A strong Design card means the idea is now packaged well enough to test.

`AISearchRank` is a good Design-stage example because:
- the problem is understandable quickly
- the target buyer is clear enough
- the product has a name and domain
- there is a PRD
- there is a mockup
- there is a meta prompt/build brief
- validation fields are already present for the next step

### Key lesson
Design should reduce ambiguity.
If another agent looks at the card, they should understand what would be tested next.

## Example 4 — Strong Validation Card

A strong Validation card should show real market testing, not just a placeholder.

### Good pattern
A good Validation card would contain:
- LP URL: live landing page
- Signups: actual count
- Qualified: count from the right audience
- Conversion: real percentage
- Traffic Source: where demand came from
- Last Check: recent date
- Validation Status: a clear state such as TESTING / STRONG / WEAK

### Good example shape
- LP URL: `https://example.com`
- Signups: `34`
- Qualified: `11`
- Conversion: `12.4%`
- Traffic Source: `Reddit + direct outreach`
- Last Check: `2026-05-22`
- Validation Status: `PROMISING`

This tells another agent something real happened.

## Example 5 — Weak Validation Card

A weak Validation card often looks active but says very little.

### Weak pattern
- LP URL: live
- Signups: 47
- Qualified: unknown
- Conversion: unknown
- Traffic Source: unknown
- Last Check: missing
- Validation Status: testing

Why this is weak:
- signups alone are not enough
- there is no idea whether the right people responded
- there is no signal quality
- no meaningful decision can be made from it

## Example 6 — Strong Build Decision

A project should move to Build when evidence supports it.

A strong Build move usually looks like:
- repeated qualified signups
- buyers replying with real need
- some willingness-to-pay evidence
- message-market fit looks real enough to justify implementation

### Good reasoning
"This moved to Build because the landing page converted, multiple target buyers replied, and the interest came from the right audience."

### Bad reasoning
"This moved to Build because the mockup looks great and we like the idea."

## Example 7 — Strong Kill Decision

A strong kill decision is evidence-based, not emotional.

### Good kill reasoning
- the wrong audience responded
- validation traffic converted poorly
- urgency was weak
- incumbents solved the problem well enough
- buyer willingness to pay looked weak

### Good posture
Killing a weak idea is a win for the system.
It protects attention for stronger opportunities.
