# VALIDATION_PLAYBOOK.md

This document explains how to validate a Design-stage idea before moving it to Build.

## Goal

The goal of Validation is to answer:
**Will enough of the right people show real interest in this product before it exists?**

Validation is not vanity.
It is a demand test.

## What Validation Requires

A project should not enter Validation unless it has:
- clear positioning
- a simple landing page concept
- a CTA
- a way to capture signups or replies
- a target audience for distribution

## Minimum Landing Page Components

A validation page should usually have:
- a strong headline
- a clear pain statement
- a direct promise/outcome
- a visual or mockup
- a simple CTA
- email capture or request-for-access form

Optional but useful:
- a qualification question
- company name field
- current workaround question
- willingness-to-pay indicator

## Good CTAs

Better CTAs focus on intent, not vague interest.

Examples:
- Get early access
- Join the waitlist
- Request a sample
- Get notified when this launches
- Ask for a demo

## Distribution Principles

Send the landing page to places where the pain already exists.

Good channels:
- Reddit threads about the problem
- niche communities
- founder/operator groups
- direct outreach to likely buyers
- social posts where target users already discuss the issue

## Forum Posting Standard

Do not be deceptive.

Avoid fake posts that pretend the product is already established.

Better framing:
- "I kept seeing this problem, so I mocked up a lightweight tool for it — useful or not?"
- "Testing a simple product for this workflow. Want early access?"
- "Built a quick concept because current options are too expensive. Would this help?"

The goal is honest validation, not spam.

## What to Track

For Validation-stage cards, keep these fields current:
- LP URL
- Signups
- Qualified
- Conversion
- Traffic Source
- Last Check
- Validation Status

## What Counts as a Qualified Signup

A qualified signup is not just any email.

A stronger signup usually means the person:
- fits the target buyer profile
- clearly understands the problem
- is likely to use the product
- optionally shares workflow context
- optionally shows urgency or willingness to pay

## Strong Validation Signals

Examples of strong signals:
- qualified signups from the right audience
- direct replies saying "I need this"
- requests for launch date
- requests for demo or pilot
- clear willingness-to-pay statements
- high conversion from relevant traffic

## Weak Validation Signals

Examples of weak signals:
- random curiosity clicks
- signups from the wrong audience
- compliments with no intent
- lots of traffic with poor conversion
- vague interest but no reply or follow-through

## Move to Build When

A project should move from Validation to Build when there is enough signal that building it is justified.

This can include:
- meaningful qualified signup count
- strong reply quality
- clear pilot interest
- good conversion from relevant traffic
- willingness-to-pay evidence

There is no universal threshold, but the evidence should make the build decision feel grounded, not hopeful.

## Kill or Hold When

Hold or kill a project if:
- traffic is relevant but conversion is weak
- wrong people are signing up
- buyers do not respond to the promise
- there is no urgency
- existing options are good enough after all
- distribution is harder than expected relative to upside

## Validation Output Standard

At the end of validation, another agent should be able to answer:
- what page was tested
- who saw it
- how people responded
- whether the interest was qualified
- whether the project deserves to move to Build
